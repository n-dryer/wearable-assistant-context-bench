Model Evaluation for an AI Wearable Device

How I built the Wearable Assistant Context Benchmark to answer a real product question (which multimodal model do we ship when the user's situation keeps changing) and the four decisions that made the answer defensible.

The problem

The user's situation keeps changing. The model doesn't follow.

Two failures, both observed in product testing of multimodal coaching assistants:

Tool swap. A user asks about a hammer they're holding. They put it down, pick up a screwdriver, then ask, "how do I use this?" The assistant answers about the hammer.
Room change. A user asks about the bedroom walls. They walk into the kitchen and ask about the walls again. The assistant answers as if the user is still in the bedroom.

The pattern: the user's situation changed between turns. The assistant didn't follow. The user has to restate context the assistant should have inferred from what it can see and hear.

This is a model-selection problem, not a prompting problem. Some models stay anchored to the prior context; some update; the gap between them is product-defining for an in-the-moment coaching assistant. One-off vibes-checks across two or three models on two or three demos isn't enough to make a buying call. Every candidate needs to be tested on the same scenarios, with the same prompt conditions, the same judge rules, and a saved run record.

So I built one.

Approach

Why a benchmark, and why this one

Existing multimodal benchmarks measure perception or reasoning in isolation. They don't measure whether a model re-grounds after a situation shift, which is the failure mode that breaks coaching assistants in the wild. I needed a controlled comparison surface I could re-run on every candidate: a fixed-content scenario bank, deterministic by construction and reproducible from the manifest.

The constraint: I'm a PM, not a research lab. The benchmark had to be small enough to author with editorial control and narrow enough to defend. The numbers had to be sharp enough to act on.

What that meant

  • A 50-scenario bank, hand-authored to a 10-point checklist plus four programmatic validation checks (token leakage, object-name leakage, schema, duplication) and locked by SHA256 manifest.
  • Three turns per scenario: Turn 1 establishes the situation, Turn 2 fires after a shift, scoring is on Turn 2.
  • An LLM judge (different family from the candidate by default) labels each Turn 2 response as current (right frame), prior (wrong frame), clarify, or abstain.
  • The primary score is balanced accuracy across current and prior, macro-averaged so a model that always answers from the latest frame can't game the headline.
Measurement

How it measures what it measures

The construct is context tracking: whether the model uses the latest situational evidence visible in the camera channel, or stays anchored to an earlier frame. The scenario format encodes the construct directly.

Three turns, scored on the second

Each scenario has Turn 1 (initial question grounded in scene A), Turn 2 (follow-up after a context shift to scene B, visible only in the camera channel), and an optional Turn 3 (a repair line that fires only when Turn 2 misses). The scored turn is Turn 2: the model has to register the change in the user's situation and answer about the new frame, not the old one.

Four labels, two scored classes

The judge labels each Turn 2 response as current, prior, clarify, or abstain. current and prior are the scored classes; clarify and abstain are auxiliary; with only 3 and 2 of them in the bank, per-class accuracy is too noisy to be ranking-grade.

Balanced, not raw, accuracy

The bank is 33 current scenarios and 12 prior. A simple hit rate would let a model that always answers from the latest frame score ≥66% by ignoring the prior class entirely. That's exactly the failure mode the benchmark exists to detect. Balancing forces the candidate to handle both classes.

Camera-channel ablation
60.6% → 14.4% (46.2 pp drop)

Same candidate (Gemini Flash-Lite), same judge, camera channel stripped out. Rules out one alternative reading: that the model is solving the task through question-phrasing patterns or memorized priors. It isn't. The camera channel is doing the work.

Combined with the per-class breakdown (87.9% on current, 33.3% on prior for the same candidate), the picture is clear: the model uses the camera, but it disproportionately grounds in the latest frame and struggles to re-anchor on an earlier one. That's the capability gap the benchmark targets.

Decisions

The four decisions that made it shippable

A benchmark is the sum of its load-bearing tradeoffs. The decisions log in the repo has eleven of these. These four carry the most weight.

Decision 1

50 scenarios, not 5,000

Authoring is the bottleneck. Every scenario passes a 10-point checklist plus four programmatic checks. Editorial control over statistical scale. The minimum detectable effect at this N is ~13 pp at 80% power; smaller deltas are inside the noise floor.

Decision 2

Class-balanced primary metric across current and prior only

The bank skews 33:12 toward current. A model that ignores prior entirely would score 66% on a hit-rate metric (high enough to look fine in a product context) but fail the exact behavior I care about. Macro-averaging refuses to let that happen.

Decision 3

Text proxies for audio and video, not raw audio and real video

The most-questioned design choice and the most defensible one. Real audio and video introduce variability in the perceptual front-end that obscures the context-tracking signal. Proxies isolate the measurement. If a model fails on text descriptions, it will fail on real video. Floor, not ceiling.

Decision 4

Cross-family judging by default + a fixed ranking judge

Same-family judges show self-preference bias (JudgeBench, MT-Bench). Cross-family judging removes it within each run. A fixed ranking judge held constant across candidates handles cross-run comparability. Two layers, not one.

Limits

What this benchmark does not measure

I'd rather surface the limits in my own voice than wait for a reviewer to find them.

Same-family judging on four of five Scenario Bank runs

API budget across providers (OpenRouter, OpenAI direct, HF Inference Pro) was exhausted mid-effort. Gemini-direct via LiteLLM was the only viable transport for the bulk of the Scenario Bank runs. Self-preference bias likely inflates the Gemini Scenario Bank scores by ~6 pp. The cross-family Qwen run is the integrity reference for the Scenario Bank. Re-running with a fixed ranking judge across all candidates is a v1.0.x follow-up.

Real video is approximated by text scene descriptions

Performance on text descriptions is not a guarantee of performance on actual video. Real-video correlation on a held-out subset is acknowledged future work.

Audio is text transcripts, not raw audio

That means none of the things real audio would let the model do — recognizing voices, hearing who's speaking to whom, picking up background sounds — get exercised.

Bank size and depth

50 scenarios across 16 activity domains. Coverage is broad but shallow. A model that crushes cooking but fails woodworking would be under-discriminated.

No human inter-annotator agreement

v1 reports cross-LLM judge agreement (Cohen's kappa = 0.443 on the adversarial run). A second human rater on a 25% sample is the strongest improvement still on the table, and the top future follow-up.

Out of scope by design

Coaching advice quality, multi-turn dynamics past three turns, proactive coaching, domain depth, latency, cost, long-horizon memory. A model that passes this benchmark still needs separate evaluation for everything in that list.

Roadmap

What I'd do next, in priority order

  1. Human inter-annotator agreement on a 25% sample. The strongest improvement still on the table.
  2. Real-video validation subset. Hold out a small representative sample, render to actual video frames, score end-to-end. Establish whether text-proxy ranking holds.
  3. Bank growth from 50 to ~150 scenarios with the same authoring discipline. Tighter CIs, deeper per-domain coverage.
  4. Full omnimodal-stack variant with live audio I/O, real-time streaming, voice-mode output. Decision-grade for deployment readiness, not just for context tracking.

I built this to answer a question I was actually facing: which model do we ship. The structure of that answer ended up being a benchmark. The construct mapping, the metric design, the same-family-judging tradeoff, the decision to use text proxies, and the future-work roadmap are PM artifacts, not researcher artifacts. The benchmark itself runs, the bank is locked by content hashes, the runner ships with CI hash-drift gates, and the findings are reproducible from the manifest.
